Abstract

 The San Francisco Bay Area is one of the world's most popular urban metropolitan regions. The curious question arises regarding the comparative analysis of the eight counties surrounding this area based on their environmental metrics and their associated health implications. To address this question, we use the CalEnviroScreen 4.0 dataset, accessible via the California open data portal. This dataset comprises environmental, health and socio-economic indicators chosen to assess the quality of life across all the 8 counties around the area.

Understanding the Dataset

Our analysis is centered around the CalEnviroScreen 4.0 dataset, a collection of environmental, health, and socio-economic data relevant to the state of California. It has  over 60 columns and 8,035 rows, presenting detailed information at the zip-code level.

The columns have a wide range of environmental indicators, such as particulate matter (PM2.5), ozone levels, and traffic density etc. It also includes health-related data like asthma, cardiovascular diseases, and low birth weight. Also, it provides  socio-economic parameters, including poverty rates and unemployment figures. For the scope of our project, we narrow the data down to the counties within the Bay Area. Below, we provide a detailed description of the selected columns.

Data Preprocessing (fully functional):

This module dealt with getting environmental and demographic datasets ready for analysis. The datasets were first combined, focusing on data from the Bay Area counties. The aim was to look at how environmental conditions relate to health, socio-economic factors, and population details. Any duplicate information in the data was removed. After cleaning and organizing the data, it was saved for use in later work.

 Linear Regression and Correlation Analysis (fully functional):

In our project, we have employed Linear Regression analysis to explore the relationships between health outcomes (as dependent variables) and various environmental indicators (as independent variables). The model is based on the assumption of a linear relationship, meaning that any change in the environmental indicators is expected to result in a proportional change in the health outcomes. After fitting the model, each environmental indicator has an associated coefficient, indicating the magnitude of its impact on health outcomes, with all other variables held constant. The effectiveness of our model is measured using R-squared and Mean Squared Error (MSE). R-squared reveals the proportion of variance in the health outcomes that can be predicted from the environmental indicators, with a value of 1 indicating perfect prediction. MSE provides an average of the squared differences between the actual and predicted health outcomes, helping us understand the accuracy of our model.

In our correlation analysis, we set multiple thresholds to identify and focus on the most significant relationships within our dataset. This approach enabled us to pinpoint the most influential environmental, health, and socio-economic factors. By exploring the correlations between these variables, we gained insights into how they are interconnected. Understanding these relationships is crucial for identifying potential areas for intervention and for guiding further analysis. The correlation analysis lays the groundwork for a deeper exploration of how environmental factors might influence health outcomes in the Bay Area, and it forms a critical component of our comprehensive assessment of the data.
